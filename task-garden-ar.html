Project File #5 — Augmented reality app

Task Garden AR

An AR task-management app that transforms tasks into a small growing garden, helping users with ADHD and executive dysfunction start, prioritize, break down, and complete tasks through guided interaction and visible progress.

Overview & Problem Space

Task Garden AR is an augmented reality app concept for Android designed to support people who struggle with task initiation, prioritization, and follow-through. For many people with ADHD, the challenge is not simply knowing what needs to be done, but beginning, choosing, and holding onto a task long enough to move it forward. Everyday productivity tools often stay too abstract, which can make large tasks feel distant, overwhelming, or difficult to start.

Concept & Core Metaphor

The project reframes productivity as growth rather than pressure. Tasks appear as seed packets, are selected through guided prioritization, opened into smaller steps, and gradually grow until they bloom, making progress feel more concrete, manageable, and rewarding.

Physical Cube Interaction System

The app is built around a physical cardboard cube that functions as an AR multi-target. Each face represents a different mode — input, prioritization, action, and reset — so rotating the cube changes context, while tapping the screen performs actions inside each stage. The user begins by loading tasks, which appear as distinct seed packets on the top face of the cube. After rotating to the prioritization side, the app helps identify which task to start first, then allows that task to be opened, broken down, watered through progress, and finally completed through a blooming stage.

Breakdown Logic & Visual Language

A key part of the concept is the connection between macro and micro support. The app does not only help the user decide what to do first, but can also expand one larger task into smaller sub-steps, making the first action more specific and less intimidating. The aesthetic is stylized and low-poly, with a soft cartoon or papercraft quality rather than realism. This approach helps the app feel more approachable and emotionally supportive, while the garden metaphor creates a calmer and more organic frame around task completion.

Why it matters & Context

This project reflects my interest in designing digital tools that help rather than simply instruct. It responds to ADHD and executive dysfunction by making invisible mental effort more visible, structured, and interactive, which aligns with my broader focus on accessibility, neurodiversity, and human-centered digital experiences. Task Garden AR explores how physical interaction, visual metaphor, and structured guidance can turn task management into something more intuitive, less abstract, and more compassionate.
