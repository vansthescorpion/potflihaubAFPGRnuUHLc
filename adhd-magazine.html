Project File #4 — Editorial design

Metamorphosis

A quarterly magazine that reimagines ADHD through the butterfly metaphor, combining accessible science communication, personal stories, and punk visual identity to challenge stereotypes and reframe the condition as transformation rather than deficit.

Concept Definition Architecture

Metamorphosis is a quarterly magazine that rethinks ADHD through the metaphor of the butterfly: from caterpillar to cocoon to butterfly, and from disorder to transformation. The project rebrands ADHD not as something broken or reduced to a deficit, but as a different operating system — one marked by intensity, sensitivity, movement, and abundance. The magazine bridges clinical knowledge and lived experience through accessible science communication, specialist interviews, personal stories, and visual metaphors.

The Core Problem

ADHD is often represented in two limiting ways: either as a childish image of hyperactivity, or as a tragic clinical failure marked by laziness, chaos, and dysfunction. What is missing is a design language that speaks to adults with ADHD who may not feel “sick,” but who experience the world through a different and often overwhelming kind of attention.

Audience Profiles

Metamorphosis is primarily aimed at Gen Z and Millennials, especially people between 18 and 40 who are curious, visually driven, or personally connected to ADHD and neurodiversity. The content is accessible to a wider age range, but the punk and Y2K visual language is especially likely to resonate with creatives, late-diagnosed adults, and readers who value authenticity over perfection. The magazine is for people who want information that is clear, visually engaging, and emotionally honest, without feeling watered down or overly clinical.

Visual Identity & System Rules

The magazine uses a square color harmony built around four main colors: a blue-leaning purple, yellow, pink, and greenish turquoise. This palette creates strong contrast, visual energy, and category coding while supporting the idea of “dopamine visual” design for ADHD readers. Black and white act as the foundation, giving the bright colors room to stand out and creating moments of rest and legibility. The layout uses a grid as a base, but breaks it intentionally to keep the pages energetic and slightly rebellious, which supports the punk character of the project.

Typography & Imagery Treatment

The magazine combines more experimental punk-inspired typography with accessible body text to balance style and readability. Because grunge and ransom-note aesthetics can become hard to read in long texts, the design keeps the more distorted fonts for titles and graphic moments, while the body copy stays legible and structured. For a print magazine, this balance matters a lot: the goal is to feel visually bold without overwhelming the reader. The page size and text scale were also chosen to feel more like a workbook, with limited text per page and enough space for each idea to breathe. The magazine uses halftone, dithering, ASCII-inspired treatments, posterization, colorization, black-and-white contrast, and overprint effects to give the visuals a raw, editorial, and slightly rebellious feel. Illustrations and graphics are intentionally messy and sketch-like, reflecting emotional intensity, childlike energy, and occasional frustration rather than polished perfection.

Structural Approaches & Interview Tracks

The magazine was designed as a horizontal publication, inspired by storybooks and workbooks, with fewer words per spread and a larger visual field. This format helps avoid overwhelm while still allowing the pages to feel rich and information-dense. The table of contents is also a design object: it is presented as a medication box, with pills containing the section titles. This makes the navigation itself part of the concept, turning something clinical into something visual and symbolic. Metamorphosis combines expert interviews, personal narratives, accessible scientific explanations, and metaphor-based communication. The project is meant to be a bridge between clinical language and lived experience, so the content can inform, challenge misconceptions, and still feel human and approachable. The magazine includes an interview with a psychologist and psychiatrist who also has ADHD, as well as research into leading ADHD voices in Portugal. One planned interview with Inês Homem de Melo could not be included in the final version because of timing, but the intent was to ground the publication in real expertise and community perspective.
